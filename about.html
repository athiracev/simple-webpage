7 WORLD WONDERS

The Colosseum - Italy, Built In 72 CE

The Colosseum, also known as the Flavian Amphitheatre, is an iconic ancient Roman structure located in Rome, Italy. It is one of the most famous and well-preserved examples of Roman architecture and engineering. Construction of the Colosseum began in 70-72 CE and was completed in 80 CE, during the Flavian dynasty. The emperors Vespasian and Titus oversaw its construction, and it was primarily used for various forms of public entertainment, such as gladiatorial contests, animal hunts, and mock naval battles. It could hold an estimated 50,000 to 80,000 spectators. The Colosseum is an elliptical amphitheater made of concrete and sand, with an outer facade largely constructed of travertine stone. It is known for its numerous arches, vaults, and columns. The structure had a complex system of elevators and trapdoors to facilitate the movement of animals, fighters, and props to the arena floor. Over the centuries, the Colosseum has suffered damage due to earthquakes, fires, and the removal of its decorative elements. Despite this, it remains a symbol of ancient Rome and is one of the most popular tourist attractions in Italy. The Colosseum has undergone extensive restoration and preservation efforts to ensure its continued existence as a cultural and historical treasure. Visitors to Rome can explore the Colosseum and learn more about its history and significance in the context of ancient Roman society and entertainment.

The Great Wall Of China - China, Built In 220 BCE

The Great Wall of China is a series of fortifications made of stone, brick, tamped earth, wood, and other materials, which were constructed across the northern border of China to protect the country from invasions by various nomadic and military incursions. It is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the most iconic symbols of China's ancient civilization. Construction of the Great Wall began much earlier than the date you mentioned. It was not built in 220 BCE but rather over several centuries. The earliest walls and fortifications were constructed as early as the 7th century BC during the Warring States period. The idea of connecting these separate walls and expanding the fortifications to create a continuous defensive barrier against northern invaders was carried out by the Qin Dynasty under the rule of Qin Shi Huang, China's first emperor, around 220-206 BCE. This was part of a larger project that also involved standardizing measures like currency, writing, and weights. The construction and maintenance of the Great Wall continued through various dynasties, including the Han, Ming, and Qing Dynasties, with different rulers and periods of expansion and repair. Over time, the Great Wall stretched for thousands of miles, with various sections built and rebuilt to protect China's borders. Different parts of the wall are made of different materials, such as brick, stone, or tamped earth. The Great Wall of China is an impressive architectural feat and is one of the most recognizable landmarks in the world. It served not only as a defense system but also as a means of controlling trade and immigration, allowing for the imposition of tariffs on goods passing through gates along the wall. Today, many sections of the Great Wall are open to tourists, offering a glimpse into China's rich history and its commitment to safeguarding its territory.

The Taj Mahal - India, Built In 1632 CE

The Taj Mahal is one of India's most iconic and world-renowned architectural marvels. It is a white marble mausoleum located in Agra, India. While your date is close, the construction of the Taj Mahal began in 1632 CE and was largely completed in 1648 CE. However, the entire complex, including the gardens and other structures, took several more years to be fully finished. The Taj Mahal was built by the Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan in memory of his favorite wife, Mumtaz Mahal, who died during childbirth. The monument is a testament to his love for her and is often considered a symbol of eternal love. The Taj Mahal is known for its exquisite architectural and artistic details. It features a central white marble dome surrounded by four minarets, all set within a large formal garden. The building's facade is adorned with intricate designs, calligraphy, and semi-precious stones. Inside the mausoleum is the cenotaph of Mumtaz Mahal and Shah Jahan, although their actual tombs are in a lower level of the structure. The Taj Mahal is not only a UNESCO World Heritage Site but also a popular tourist attraction and a symbol of India's rich history and cultural heritage. It is renowned for its stunning beauty and the meticulous craftsmanship that went into its construction, making it one of the most recognizable landmarks in the world.

Christ The Redeemer - Brazil, Built In 1926 CE

"Christ the Redeemer" is a colossal statue of Jesus Christ that overlooks the city of Rio de Janeiro, Brazil. Your date is nearly accurate, as the construction of the statue began in 1926 and was largely completed in 1931. The official dedication of the statue took place on October 12, 1931. The statue, which stands atop the Corcovado mountain in the Tijuca Forest National Park, is an iconic symbol of Rio de Janeiro and Brazil. It was designed by Brazilian engineer Heitor da Silva Costa and sculpted by French sculptor Paul Landowski. The statue is made of reinforced concrete and soapstone and stands at approximately 98 feet (30 meters) tall, with an additional 26 feet (8 meters) for its pedestal. "Christ the Redeemer" is a symbol of Christianity and a popular tourist attraction, offering breathtaking panoramic views of Rio de Janeiro and the surrounding landscape. It has become one of the New Seven Wonders of the World and is celebrated for its cultural and religious significance.

Machu Picchu - Peru, Built In 1450 CE

Machu Picchu is an ancient Inca citadel located in the Andes Mountains of Peru. Your date is quite accurate; it is believed that Machu Picchu was built around 1450 CE during the reign of the Inca emperor Pachacuti. It was constructed in the classical Inca architectural style and served as a royal estate or ceremonial retreat. Machu Picchu is known for its incredible setting, perched high in the Andes at an elevation of approximately 7,970 feet (2,430 meters) above sea level. The site includes well-preserved stone structures, terraced agricultural fields, and a sophisticated network of roads and drainage systems. It is often referred to as the "Lost City of the Incas" because it was abandoned during the Spanish conquest and remained hidden in the mountainous terrain for centuries. In 1911, American historian and explorer Hiram Bingham brought international attention to Machu Picchu when he rediscovered the site. Since then, it has become one of the most famous and visited archaeological sites in the world and is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Machu Picchu holds immense historical and cultural significance and is a testament to the advanced engineering and architectural skills of the Inca civilization. It continues to be a source of fascination and admiration for people worldwide.

Chichén Itzá - Mexico, Built In 600 CE

Chichén Itzá is an ancient Mayan archaeological site located in the Yucatan Peninsula of Mexico. While the city's history dates back to an earlier period, the major construction and development of Chichén Itzá, including the iconic pyramid known as El Castillo (or the Temple of Kukulcan), took place during the period of 600 CE and beyond. Chichén Itzá was one of the most significant cities of the Maya civilization. The site features a mix of Mayan and Toltec architectural styles, reflecting the influence and interaction between these two Mesoamerican cultures. The Temple of Kukulcan, with its impressive step-pyramid design, is a well-known symbol of Chichén Itzá and is famous for its precise astronomical alignments. The site also contains various other structures and features, including a large ballcourt, the Temple of the Warriors, the Great Cenote, and the observatory, known as El Caracol. Chichén Itzá was a hub of political, economic, and religious activity in the region during its heyday. In 1988, Chichén Itzá was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site, and it is often considered one of the New Seven Wonders of the World. It is a popular tourist destination, offering visitors a glimpse into the history, architecture, and culture of the ancient Maya civilization.

Petra - Jordan, Built In 312 BCE

Petra is an ancient archaeological city located in southern Jordan. Your date is approximately correct; while the city's history dates back even earlier, Petra reached its peak during the Hellenistic and Roman periods. The city was established as the capital of the Nabataean Kingdom in the 4th century BCE, which is close to the year 312 BCE. The Nabataeans were a nomadic Arab people who settled in the area and developed Petra as a major trading hub. The city is known for its impressive rock-cut architecture, with buildings and tombs carved directly into the rose-red cliffs of the surrounding mountains. The most famous structure in Petra is Al-Khazneh, or "The Treasury," which is a stunning monument and a popular tourist attraction. During the Roman period, Petra continued to prosper as a trading center, and the city's architecture and influence expanded. After the decline of the Nabataean Kingdom and a series of earthquakes, Petra began to decline in importance. It was eventually abandoned and largely forgotten by the Western world until it was rediscovered in the early 19th century. In 1985, Petra was designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site and is often referred to as the "Rose City" due to the color of the stone from which it is carved. It is a remarkable historical and archaeological site that offers insight into the ancient Nabataean culture and is a significant part of Jordan's cultural heritage.
